Supervised by: Yagya Raj Pandeya

Abstract

Music therapy practitioners have long employed the ISO Principle — the practice of matching a listener's current emotional state before gradually guiding them toward a therapeutic target — to facilitate emotional regulation. This paper presents Music Mood Therapy, a computational implementation of the ISO Principle that translates a user's natural-language emotional description into a three-song recommendation sequence grounded in Russell's Circumplex Model of affect. User mood text is classified by OpenAI's GPT-4o-mini into one of six Ekman emotions; the result is mapped to coordinates in Russell's two-dimensional valence–energy space; and an ISO-compliant engine selects three songs tracing a path from the user's current state toward the target emotion Happy (valence = 0.80, energy = 0.80). Each candidate song is verified in real time by a Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) trained on 373 Nepali songs across seven psychoacoustic features. A quality gate retries selection up to three times when the MLP's prediction conflicts with the expected ISO waypoint emotion. On 75 held-out test songs, the Random Forest baseline achieves 74.67% accuracy and the MLP achieves 66.67% — both substantially above the 16.67% random baseline for six-class classification. The system is deployed as a React + FastAPI web application with Docker containerisation.

Keywords — music emotion recognition, ISO Principle, Russell's Circumplex Model, MLP neural network, music therapy, librosa, Nepali music dataset, recommendation system

I. Introduction

Emotional regulation through music is one of the oldest therapeutic practices known, yet its formalisation in clinical settings is relatively recent. The ISO Principle, introduced by Altshuler [1] in psychiatric music therapy, establishes a foundational constraint: an abrupt transition from a patient's current emotional state to a target state via music creates dissonance rather than therapeutic benefit. The therapist must instead begin with material that matches the patient's affect and modulate gradually toward the therapeutic goal.

Computational music recommendation systems have historically optimised for user preference rather than emotional trajectory. Collaborative filtering and content-based systems ask what does the user typically enjoy? rather than what does the user need now, and where should the music take them? This distinction is clinically significant: a listener in distress may not benefit from their habitual listening preferences.

This paper presents Music Mood Therapy, a system that operationalises the ISO Principle as a three-step recommendation pipeline grounded in two established psychological frameworks: Russell's Circumplex Model of affect [2] and Ekman's taxonomy of six basic emotions [3]. The system accepts a free-text emotional description, classifies it using a large language model (LLM), maps the result to a coordinate in Russell's two-dimensional valence–energy space, and selects three songs that trace an ISO-compliant path toward the target emotion (Happy, at valence = 0.80, energy = 0.80).

Song selection is validated in real time by an MLP neural network trained on 373 Nepali songs with seven acoustic features extracted using librosa [4]. A quality gate mechanism retries song selection if the MLP's live classification conflicts with the expected emotional category at each waypoint.

The primary contributions of this work are:

  1. A computational implementation of the ISO Principle using Russell's Circumplex as the coordinate system for emotional trajectory planning.
  2. A curated dataset of 373 Nepali songs with emotion labels derived from human-curated Spotify playlists and supplementary local recordings.
  3. An MLP-based quality gate for ISO-coherent recommendation operating on seven psychoacoustic features extracted entirely via open-source tooling.
  4. A full-stack web application and CLI enabling real-time mood-to-music recommendation and standalone audio emotion classification.

II. Related Work

A. The ISO Principle in Clinical Music Therapy

The ISO Principle was first described by Altshuler [1] in the context of psychiatric treatment, where matching a patient's observed affect with congruent music was found to facilitate engagement and subsequent emotional shift. Thaut and Davis [5] provided empirical support, demonstrating that state-congruent music selection produced superior therapeutic outcomes relative to experimenter-assigned material. Our work formalises this principle computationally, replacing the therapist's perceptual judgement with acoustic feature matching in Russell's coordinate space.

B. Russell's Circumplex Model of Affect

Russell [2] proposed mapping emotional states onto a circular structure defined by two orthogonal dimensions: valence (hedonic quality, from unpleasant to pleasant on the X-axis) and arousal (activation level, from low to high energy on the Y-axis). This model has become the predominant framework for affective computing in music, providing a continuous, mathematically tractable representation of emotion suitable for distance calculations. We adopt Russell's model as the coordinate system for ISO waypoint computation, mapping each Ekman emotion to an empirically motivated (valence, energy) coordinate pair.

C. Ekman's Basic Emotions

Ekman [3] established through cross-cultural research that six emotions — Happiness, Sadness, Anger, Fear, Disgust, and Surprise — are universally recognised. This discrete label set aligns with clinical categories used in music therapy literature and with the resolution achievable by acoustic feature-based classifiers on small datasets.

D. Music Emotion Recognition

Feature-based classifiers operating on spectral, rhythmic, and tonal features have demonstrated competitive performance relative to deep learning approaches when training data is limited. Panda et al. [6] identify mode (major/minor) and tempo as among the most discriminative features for valence prediction, consistent with our feature set. The Krumhansl–Schmuckler key-finding algorithm [7], which we employ for mode detection, has been validated as a reliable computational implementation of Western tonal hierarchy.

III. Dataset Construction

A. Primary Collection — 70 Songs (Human-Labeled)

The primary dataset was constructed by identifying six Spotify playlists curated by human editors around emotion categories corresponding to Ekman's taxonomy. Track metadata and Spotify track identifiers were extracted from each playlist. Since Spotify does not provide audio file access, corresponding audio was retrieved from YouTube using yt-dlp via a custom Python pipeline. Librosa [4] extracted seven acoustic features from each downloaded audio file. Emotion labels were assigned by playlist category — constituting human-curated labels fully independent of the acoustic features used as model inputs.

B. Supplementary Collection — 303 Songs (Rule-Labeled)

A supplementary set was derived from 597 locally recorded Nepali songs. The same librosa feature extraction pipeline produced the seven-feature representation for each recording. Emotion labels were assigned via rule-based thresholds derived from Russell's Circumplex coordinates: songs with valence > 0.60 and energy > 0.60 were labeled Happy; valence < 0.40 and energy < 0.40 labeled Sad; and so forth, with the nearest Circumplex centroid as a fallback.

Two filters controlled class imbalance: (i) emotion classes with fewer than ten songs were discarded as insufficiently represented for learning; (ii) classes exceeding 100 songs were capped at 100 to prevent majority-class dominance. This reduced the 597-song raw collection to 303 songs.

C. Combined Dataset

The two collections were merged and deduplicated by track identifier, yielding 373 songs total. A stratified 80/20 train-test split produced 298 training and 75 test samples, with stratification preserving class proportions across the split. Table I shows the final emotion distribution.

IV. Methodology

A. Feature Extraction

Seven acoustic features are extracted from each audio file using librosa [4]. Identical extraction code is applied during dataset construction and live inference, ensuring no train–inference mismatch.

Valence is not directly measurable from audio. We use a psychoacoustic approximation grounded in prior literature [6]: major key, faster tempo, and higher spectral brightness each independently contribute to perceived positivity.

valence = clip( 0.40 × mode + 0.35 × tempo_norm + 0.25 × centroid_norm, 0.05, 0.95 )
where tempo_norm = clip( (BPM − 60) / 120, 0, 1 )
       centroid_norm = clip( spectral_centroid / 4000, 0, 1 )

B. MLP Neural Network

A Multi-Layer Perceptron is trained within a scikit-learn Pipeline [8] that applies StandardScaler normalisation before each forward pass. Normalisation is essential: raw feature magnitudes differ by orders of magnitude (tempo: 60–180 BPM; valence: 0–1), and unscaled inputs cause tempo to dominate gradient updates.

Architecture: 7 inputs → StandardScaler → 32 (ReLU) → 16 (ReLU) → 6 outputs (softmax)
Learnable parameters ≈ 886  |  Training samples: 298  |  Ratio ≈ 3 samples / parameter

Architecture depth and width were chosen deliberately for the dataset scale. A 128→64 network would yield approximately 9,700 learnable parameters for 298 training samples — roughly 32 parameters per sample — presenting high overfitting risk. The 32→16 architecture reduces this to approximately 886 parameters, a ratio of roughly three samples per parameter. Early stopping monitors validation loss on a 20% holdout of the training set, terminating training when improvement plateaus.

C. Random Forest Baseline

A Random Forest of 100 decision trees is trained with class_weight='balanced' to compensate for class imbalance. Each tree trains on a bootstrap sample and considers a random feature subset at each split. The balanced weighting ensures minority classes (Sad, Disgust) receive proportional gradient signal. The Random Forest is not used in the live recommendation pipeline because it does not natively produce calibrated probability distributions across all classes — a requirement for the quality gate.

D. Mood Parsing via LLM

User mood text is classified into one of six Ekman emotion labels by OpenAI's GPT-4o-mini via a zero-shot structured prompt at temperature = 0 (deterministic output). The model is constrained to return exactly one label with no additional text. This separation of natural language understanding from acoustic classification allows each component to operate at the resolution it is best suited for. Approximate cost per request: $0.0001 USD (10 output tokens at GPT-4o-mini pricing).

E. ISO Recommendation Engine

Given a detected emotion e with Russell coordinate (ve, ene), the engine retrieves three songs following the ISO Principle:

MATCH → target: (v_e, en_e) top_k = 20
BRIDGE → target: ((v_e + 0.80)/2, (en_e + 0.80)/2) top_k = 15
DESTINATION → target: (0.80, 0.80) [Happy] top_k = 10

At each waypoint, Euclidean distance in (valence, energy) space identifies the nearest songs. One is sampled uniformly at random from the top-K candidates, ensuring variety across sessions for the same input emotion. Progressively smaller candidate pools at later waypoints enforce greater specificity as the journey approaches the therapeutic target.

F. MLP Quality Gate

After candidate selection at each waypoint, the MLP classifies the candidate using its stored seven-feature vector. If the predicted emotion conflicts with the waypoint's expected label (MATCH expects e; DESTINATION expects Happy; BRIDGE carries no constraint), the engine samples the next nearest unvisited candidate — up to three attempts. If no agreeing candidate is found within three attempts, the most recent candidate is accepted to ensure response completeness. This gate operationalises the ISO requirement for emotional coherence at each step of the journey.

A. Classification Performance

Both models substantially outperform the six-class random baseline of 16.67%. The accuracy gap between Random Forest and MLP is consistent with the known advantage of ensemble tree methods over neural networks on small, tabular datasets — bootstrapped ensembles reduce variance more effectively when training samples are limited.

B. Class Imbalance and Rare Emotions

Sad and Disgust achieve near-zero recall under both models. This is a data scarcity problem: no classifier can reliably learn a six-dimensional boundary from eight to eleven examples. Balanced weighting in the Random Forest and early stopping in the MLP both mitigate but cannot resolve this fundamental constraint. Expansion of the Nepali dataset for underrepresented emotion categories is the primary direction for future work.

C. Comparison With Kaggle Baseline

An initial system trained on the Kaggle Spotify dataset (89,000 songs) with rule-derived emotion labels achieved greater than 99% training accuracy. This figure is artefactual: Kaggle labels were assigned by thresholding the same acoustic features used as model inputs, producing circular validation. Labels assigned independently of features — as in our human-curated primary dataset — represent a substantially more challenging and honest evaluation. The 74.67% and 66.67% figures reported here reflect genuine generalisation on unseen Nepali songs.

VI. Conclusion

This work presents a computational implementation of the ISO Principle for music-based emotional regulation, grounding the recommendation pipeline in Russell's Circumplex Model and Ekman's emotion taxonomy. A curated dataset of 373 Nepali songs with human-assigned emotion labels was constructed via a reproducible yt-dlp + librosa pipeline. An MLP neural network achieving 66.67% accuracy on held-out data serves as a live quality gate during recommendation, with a Random Forest baseline achieving 74.67%. Both models perform 4–4.5× above the random baseline, representing genuine generalisation on independently labeled data.

Limitations include the small dataset size, near-zero recall for Sad and Disgust categories, and the absence of formal user studies validating therapeutic efficacy. Future work will focus on dataset expansion for underrepresented emotion classes, user-study evaluation of emotional transition outcomes, and exploration of self-supervised audio representations to reduce dependence on hand-crafted features.
